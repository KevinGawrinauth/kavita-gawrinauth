Devotional Music

Teree Karam Kahaanee

← Back to Music

The classic 1971 song Teree Karam Kahaanee explores the idea that a person’s conscience and the Supreme Being already know the truth of one’s actions, regardless of how carefully those actions are concealed from others. Through its reflections on karma, repentance, and spiritual accountability, the song encourages listeners to examine their conduct and recognize that every action carries consequences.

Although Teree Karam Kahaanee originated in Hindi cinema, its devotional and philosophical message allowed it to acquire a life beyond the film. Hindi films have long incorporated bhajans, prayers, sacred imagery, and songs addressing moral and spiritual questions. Many of these songs continued to be remembered and performed after audiences first encountered them through cinema, radio, records, and cassette tapes. In homes, mandirs, religious gatherings, and family performances, devotional film songs could become part of a wider living repertoire of sacred music.

This relationship between cinema and devotional practice has been especially meaningful within Indo-Guyanese communities. Hindi films and recordings provided access to religious language, melodies, images, and stories associated with India. Songs learned through cinema could be remembered, performed, and passed between relatives, musicians, mandirs, and generations. Over time, the distinction between a traditional bhajan and a devotional song originating in a film could become less important than the spiritual meaning the song acquired through repeated performance.

Although many Indo-Guyanese people today do not speak or fully understand Hindi, Hindi-language devotional songs continue to be enjoyed, remembered, and sung. This does not necessarily suggest an innate knowledge of the language. Rather, it points to a form of aural and embodied cultural memory developed through repeated listening and participation. Words and melodies encountered through films, bhajans, mandir worship, recordings, and family gatherings can become deeply familiar even when their precise meanings are not fully understood.

Through repetition, singers may learn to reproduce the sounds, rhythms, and emotional qualities of Hindi devotional songs without using Hindi in everyday conversation. The language remains present through the ear, voice, body, and memory. Singing can therefore become a way of inhabiting a cultural and spiritual inheritance that extends beyond ordinary speech. Hindi survives not only as spoken language, but also as sacred sound, family memory, and an emotional connection to India.

This continuing attachment to devotional sound belongs to a longer Indo-Guyanese history beginning with Indian indenture. Indians who crossed the kala pani from India to British Guiana carried religious practices, sacred stories, oral traditions, and forms of devotional singing with them. They were not yet Indo-Guyanese, but through migration, settlement, worship, and everyday cultural practice, new Indo-Guyanese identities gradually emerged.

In Guyana, these inherited traditions were preserved, adapted, and expanded over generations. Hindi cinema later introduced additional songs, melodies, and religious ideas into an already living devotional culture. As Indo-Guyanese families migrated from Guyana to New York and elsewhere, they carried both older inherited traditions and songs absorbed through cinema, recordings, mandirs, and family practice.

Teree Karam Kahaanee reflects this continuing process. Its themes of karma, conscience, repentance, and divine knowledge allowed it to move beyond its original cinematic setting and become spiritually meaningful through repeated performance. Performing this song today connects several stages of cultural movement: from India to British Guiana under indenture, from Guyana to New York through later migration, and from Hindi cinema into Indo-Guyanese family and devotional life.

Featured Recording

Teree Karam Kahaanee

“The Story of Your Actions”

Originally sung by Manna Dey for the 1971 Hindi film Chingari.

Lyrics by Sahir Ludhianvi; music by Ravi.

Performed by Kavita Gawrinauth on voice and harmonium, with tabla accompaniment by Kevin Gawrinauth.

Lyrics and Translation

Hindi Lyrics and English Translation

The translation below conveys the devotional and philosophical meaning of the song as it is performed. The lyrics follow the transliterated version used in this performance and may differ from standardized Hindi spelling or other recorded versions.

Praanee re, praanee re
Teree karam kahaanee
Teree atmaa bhee jane, Parmaatmaa bhee jaane
Kahäa chhupegaa re praanee
Teree aatmaa bhee jaane, Parmaatmaa bhee jaane

O human being, this is the story of your actions. Your own soul knows what you have done, and the Supreme Being knows as well. Where can you hide? Both your soul and God know the story of your life.

Kitne hi pardö me, paap kamaaye too
Kitne hi jatanö se, bhed chhupaaye too
Bhale bure ki kahaani, praanee re
Teree aatmaa bhee jane – Parmaatmaa bhee jaane

You have committed many wrongful actions under cover and have made many efforts to conceal them. Yet both your own soul and the Supreme Being know the story of the good and bad actions you have committed.

Jag jise kehte haï, karmö kee khetee hai
Jaisaa koi boye, use waisaa phal detee hai
Too-ne boyaa kyaa thaa praanee, O…..
Teree aatmaa bhee jaane – Parmaatmaa bhee jaane

The world is like a field in which actions are planted as seeds. Whatever a person sows, that person eventually harvests. What have you sown in your life? Both your soul and the Supreme Being know.

Prabhoo ke du-aare teraa, aanaa hee bahut hai
Bol ke na bol, pachh-taanaa hee bahut hai
Jo hai vipadaa sunaanee, praanee re
Teree aatmaa bhee jaane – Parmaatmaa bhee jaane

Simply coming before the Lord is itself meaningful. Whether or not you speak your actions aloud, sincere repentance is what matters. Whatever sorrow, wrongdoing, or adversity you wish to acknowledge is already known to your soul and to the Supreme Being.

Reflection

Film, Faith, and Diasporic Continuity

Teree Karam Kahaanee asks listeners to examine the moral consequences of their actions and to recognize that wrongdoing cannot be hidden from either the individual soul or the Supreme Being. Its image of the world as a field of karma suggests that every action is a seed whose consequences must eventually be faced.

The song therefore presents spiritual life as more than prayer, praise, or refuge. It also emphasizes ethical responsibility. Faith requires individuals to confront their actions, acknowledge their mistakes, and choose a more righteous path. In this sense, devotional music can encourage not only comfort, but also humility, accountability, repentance, and moral transformation.

For me, the song also demonstrates how devotional traditions survive through adaptation. Its origins in Hindi cinema do not diminish its spiritual meaning. Instead, its continued performance shows how communities receive songs from different sources and incorporate them into religious, cultural, and family life.

Through voice, harmonium, tabla, and dholak, songs inherited through family, community, and Hindi cinema become part of a shared Indo-Guyanese devotional repertoire in New York. The journey of Teree Karam Kahaanee from Indian cinema into Indo-Guyanese performance illustrates how tradition lives not by remaining unchanged, but by traveling, adapting, and acquiring new meanings. It connects India, Guyana, and New York while demonstrating how sound can preserve memory, sustain faith, and carry cultural belonging across oceans and generations.
